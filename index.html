Paired t-Test

Evaluate sales training program effectiveness using dependent samples — the same 30 salespeople measured before and after.

Business scenario: A $500/person sales training was deployed. Monthly sales ($000s) were recorded for 30 salespeople before and after training. The fundamental methodological question: why must we treat these as paired observations rather than two independent groups?
The pairing principle — intuition first
1
Between-person variation dominates. Salesperson A naturally sells $46K/mo; Person B sells $38K/mo. This spread reflects territories, experience, and talent — it is irrelevant noise when asking "did training work?" An independent t-test mixes this noise into its error term.
2
Differencing eliminates the noise. Comparing each person's after minus their before (d_i = after_i − before_i) cancels individual baseline effects. The analysis focuses purely on within-person change — the quantity we actually care about.
3
Statistical consequence: higher power. Pairing reduces σ_diff compared to σ_pooled. When the pre-post correlation r is high (common for human performance data), the efficiency gain is 1/(1−r²). At r = 0.8, pairing is 2.78× more powerful than an independent t-test with the same n.

Paired t-test results
# One-tailed test: training should improve sales H₀: μ_diff = 0 # Training has no effect Hₐ: μ_diff > 0 # Training increases sales (one-tailed) t_stat, p_val = scipy.stats.ttest_rel(sales_after, sales_before) # Result: t → ∞ (SD_diff = 0), p < 0.0001 # 95% CI for mean difference: [$3.00K, $3.00K]

Statistical conclusion: We reject H₀. The training produces a statistically significant improvement in monthly sales. Every salesperson improved by exactly $3K — the variance of differences is zero, making t → ∞ and p → 0.

Note for real data: In practice, individual responses vary. The paired t-test remains appropriate regardless; the test simply has a finite t-statistic when SD_diff > 0.

Chi-Square Test

Test whether customer product preferences are independent of geographic region — a critical question for regional marketing strategy.

Business scenario: 450 customers across 3 regions (North, South, West) expressed preferences for Economy, Standard, or Premium product variants. Does region drive product preference, or are the patterns consistent nationwide?
What does statistical independence mean here?
H₀
Independence: Knowing a customer's region gives no information about their product preference. The proportion choosing Premium is the same in North, South, and West. A uniform national product strategy is optimal.
Hₐ
Dependence / Association: Some regions systematically prefer Premium; others favor Economy. Regional marketing and inventory strategies should differ to match local preferences.
The chi-square logic
# What would frequencies look like if NOTHING differed by region? E_ij = (Row_total_i × Col_total_j) / Grand_total χ² = Σ (Observed − Expected)² / Expected # Large χ² → observed deviations unlikely under H₀ → reject independence χ², p, dof, expected = scipy.stats.chi2_contingency(preference_data) # Effect size: Cramér's V = √(χ² / (n × min(r−1, c−1)))

ANOVA — partitioning total variance

Statistical conclusion: F(3,44) = 89.7, p < 0.001. We reject H₀. At least one region mean is significantly different from the others.

What ANOVA cannot tell us: The omnibus test only confirms "something differs." It does not identify which specific regions differ. Without post-hoc tests, we cannot say whether Northeast outperforms Midwest, or whether Southeast is uniquely poor. Tukey HSD is required.

Concepts & Quiz

Consolidate your understanding before submitting the homework. Five applied graduate-level questions.

Decision framework — which inference test fits your problem?
?
What type of outcome variable? If numeric/continuous → t-test or ANOVA. If counts in categories → Chi-square. This is the first split.
?
How many groups? Two groups → t-test. Three or more → one-way ANOVA. Never run repeated pairwise t-tests — familywise Type I error explodes.
?
Independent or paired/matched data? Same subjects measured twice, or matched pairs → paired t. Different subjects in each group → independent t or ANOVA.

t-tests (both types)
1
Independence — observations do not influence each other within or between groups.
2
Normality — each group is approximately normal. Relax with n ≥ 30 (CLT). Check with Q-Q plot or Shapiro-Wilk.
3
Equal variance (independent t only) — Levene's test. If violated, use Welch's t (equal_var=False in scipy).
ANOVA
1
Independence — within and between all groups.
2
Normality — each group approximately normal. Q-Q plots for each region.
3
Homogeneity of variance — Levene's test across all groups simultaneously (not pairwise).
Chi-square (one critical assumption)

Expected cell frequencies ≥ 5 in every cell. This is non-negotiable. If violated: (1) combine sparse categories, (2) use Fisher's exact test for 2×2 tables, or (3) collect more data. Violations make the χ² approximation unreliable regardless of observed counts.
